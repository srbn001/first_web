Buddhism in Sikkim

Exploring the rich spiritual heritage and cultural significance of Buddhism in the Himalayan kingdom of Sikkim

History of Buddhism in Sikkim

Buddhism was introduced to Sikkim around the 8th century by the revered Buddhist saint Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava), who is said to have blessed the land and predicted the establishment of Buddhism in the region. However, it was in the 17th century that Buddhism became firmly established with the arrival of three Tibetan lamas who consecrated Phuntsog Namgyal as the first Chogyal (religious king) of Sikkim in 1642.

The Nyingma and Kagyu schools of Tibetan Buddhism were predominant in the early years. In the 18th and 19th centuries, many monasteries were established throughout Sikkim, becoming centers of learning, culture, and spiritual practice. Following the Chinese occupation of Tibet in the 1950s, many Tibetan lamas sought refuge in Sikkim, further enriching the Buddhist traditions in the region.

"Sikkim is a hidden jewel of Vajrayana Buddhism, where ancient traditions are preserved and practiced with devotion."

Today, Buddhism is an integral part of Sikkimese culture and identity, with the majority of the population practicing this ancient religion. The state government actively supports the preservation and promotion of Buddhist heritage, recognizing its importance to the cultural fabric of Sikkim.

Famous Monasteries of Sikkim

Sikkim is home to over 200 monasteries, each with its own unique history and spiritual significance. These gompas (monasteries) are not just places of worship but also centers of learning, art, and culture.

Rumtek Monastery

Often called the 'Dharma Chakra Centre', Rumtek is one of the most significant monasteries in Sikkim. It is the seat of the Karmapa, the head of the Karma Kagyu school. Established in the 16th century, it was rebuilt in the 1960s and houses some of the rarest Buddhist religious artifacts.

Pemayangtse Monastery

One of the oldest monasteries in Sikkim, Pemayangtse was established in 1705 and belongs to the Nyingma school. The monastery is famous for its seven-tiered wooden structure depicting the heavenly abode of Guru Rinpoche.

Tashiding Monastery

Perched on a hilltop between the Rathong and Rangeet rivers, Tashiding is considered one of the holiest monasteries in Sikkim. The sacred 'Bhumchu' ceremony held here annually attracts thousands of devotees.

Buddhist Festivals of Sikkim

The Buddhist calendar in Sikkim is marked by numerous festivals that celebrate the teachings of Buddha, important events in Buddhist history, and the changing seasons.

Losar

Losar is the Tibetan New Year celebration, marked by prayers, rituals, and feasting. Monasteries are adorned with colorful decorations, and cham dances (religious mask dances) are performed by monks.

Saga Dawa

This triple blessed festival commemorates the birth, enlightenment, and parinirvana of Buddha. Devotees visit monasteries, offer prayers, and engage in acts of generosity.

Pang Lhabsol

Unique to Sikkim, this festival was introduced by the third Chogyal to commemorate the sworn brotherhood between the Lepchas and Bhutias. It also honors Mount Kanchenjunga, the guardian deity of Sikkim.
